Key terms: stock, share, market

Stock vs. Share

A stock is the overall equity of a company traded in the market; a share is a single unit of ownership of that stock (e.g., owning 100 shares of Company A’s stock). Common stock usually includes voting rights and potential dividends; preferred stock typically has priority dividends but limited voting rights.

The Stock Market

The stock market is a network of exchanges and electronic venues where buyers and sellers trade company shares, facilitating price discovery and capital raising via IPOs and secondary offerings. Markets match orders and report trades to maintain transparency and liquidity.

How the stock market works

Primary vs. Secondary Markets

In primary markets, companies sell new shares to investors (e.g., IPOs) to raise capital; in secondary markets, investors trade existing shares with each other, and prices fluctuate based on supply, demand, and expectations.

Order Types

Common orders include market orders (execute immediately at current prices) and limit orders (execute at a specified price or better), plus stop/stop‑limit for risk control and trailing stops for dynamic exits.

Indices

Indices like broad or sector benchmarks track groups of stocks to indicate market or segment performance, and are widely used as passive investment targets via index funds and ETFs.

Returns & Taxes

Investor returns come from price appreciation and dividends; total return combines both. Tax treatment varies by jurisdiction and account type; planning often pairs strategy with tax‑efficient vehicles.

Valuation metrics

P/E Ratio

The price‑to‑earnings ratio compares a company’s share price to its earnings per share; it’s used to gauge valuation versus history, peers, and the market, noting growth prospects and cyclicality.

Other Metrics

Additional metrics include price‑to‑book (P/B), price‑to‑sales (P/S), dividend yield and payout ratio, return on equity (ROE), and free cash flow yield for assessing efficiency, quality, and income.

Tip: Compare a company’s valuation to its sector and its own history, not just to the overall market.

Strategies to select stocks

ApproachWhat it focuses onWhen it helps
Value Buying below estimated intrinsic value using metrics like P/E, P/B, FCF yield. Mean‑reversion markets; when pessimism overshoots fundamentals.
Growth Revenue/EPS growth, TAM, margins, reinvestment runway, competitive moats. Structural innovation cycles and secular trends.
Dividend/Income Dividend yield, payout safety, growth consistency, cash flow coverage. Income needs and lower‑volatility objectives.
Indexing Low‑cost broad diversification via index funds/ETFs to match the market. Long‑term compounding with minimal selection risk.
Factor Blends Rules‑based tilts like quality, value, momentum, low‑volatility. Systematic diversification across drivers of returns.

Fundamental Analysis

Study financial statements, unit economics, industry structure, and management quality to estimate intrinsic value and risk‑adjusted return potential over a multi‑year horizon.

Technical & Sentiment

Use price/volume trends, moving averages, support/resistance, and market breadth to inform timing and risk, complementing—but not replacing—fundamentals.

Risks and how to reduce them

Major Risk Types

  • Systematic (market) risk from macro shocks, rates, and geopolitics affects most stocks simultaneously.
  • Unsystematic (company/industry) risk stems from idiosyncratic events like competition, execution, or governance.
  • Liquidity and operational risks include wide spreads, thin volume, platform outages, and cyber issues.

Risk Reduction Toolkit

  • Diversification across sectors, styles, and regions lowers single‑name and sector concentration risk.
  • Asset allocation blends stocks with bonds/cash/alternatives; periodic rebalancing maintains target risk.
  • Position sizing and the 1–2% per‑position risk rule help cap drawdowns; stops/alerts enforce discipline.
  • Dollar‑cost averaging (DCA) smooths entry timing risk by investing fixed amounts on a schedule.
  • Due diligence on financials, valuation, and quality reduces avoidable idiosyncratic risks.

Building and maintaining a portfolio

Starting Steps

Define goals and horizon, pick a broker, choose a core approach (indexing or a diversified core) and add active tilts only with clear rationale and risk limits.

Ongoing Process

Schedule reviews to rebalance, evaluate thesis changes, and update risk controls; keep costs and taxes in view to protect net returns over time.

Beginner checklist

  • Write down objective, horizon, and risk tolerance; choose an approach (index core plus optional tilts).
  • Automate contributions (DCA), diversify broadly, and cap single‑name weights.
  • Use limits for entries, define exits, and set max loss per position/portfolio.
  • Review/rebalance at a fixed cadence; avoid strategy drift from short‑term noise.

Mini‑glossary

Market Cap

Share price × shares outstanding; rough measure of company size and market value.

Dividend Yield

Annual dividend ÷ share price; income rate that must be weighed against payout safety and growth.

Volatility

Degree of price fluctuation; affects drawdowns and required risk controls in portfolio design.
